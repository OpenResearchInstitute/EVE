Scientific Justification

Earth-Venus-Earth radar experiments have produced foundational planetary
science. The professional astronomy EVE campaigns of the 1960s and 1970s
established Venus's rotation period, retrograde rotation direction, and
initial results on surface roughness and radar albedo. The 2012 Arecibo/GBT
bistatic radar campaign produced high-resolution surface maps that remain a
primary reference for Venus geology. The disk-integrated radar albedo of
Venus is well characterized at 1-2 GHz at approximately 0.152 (Goldstein &
Carpenter 1963; Evans 1968), but no ground-based measurements at 2304 MHz
exist outside of professional campaigns. The Magellan spacecraft's Global
Reflectivity Data Record (GREDR) provides surface reflectivity maps in a
compatible frequency regime, enabling quantitative comparison between
ground-based radar returns and known surface features including Maxwell
Montes (radar albedo ~0.75) and Aphrodite Terra (~0.35). ORI uses the
Magellan maps in our EVE link analysis to determine dynamic albedo.

Why is October 2026 Important?

Venus inferior conjunctions happen every 584 days. At inferior conjunction,
Venus reaches minimum Earth distance of approximately 40 million km. This
produces received signal power roughly 44 dB stronger than at the maximum
distance of 261 million km. The October 22, 2026 inferior conjunction is a
brief opportunity. The next comparable window does not occur until June 2028.

The sub-Earth longitude at the October 2026 conjunction is approximately
332 degrees East in the GREDR frame, placing the sub-radar point over plains
southwest of Aphrodite Terra. Link budget modeling using the Magellan GREDR
map yields an effective disk-integrated radar albedo of 0.1164 for this
geometry. The sub-radar longitude rotates at approximately 1.7 degrees per
day as seen from Earth, meaning each observation window samples a distinct
surface region. Multi-session observations are therefore scientifically
complementary rather than redundant.

Scientific Objectives

This experiment pursues three objectives of increasing ambition.

The primary goal is detection and characterization of the Venus radar echo
at 1299.5 MHz with substantially greater sensitivity than was available in
the March 2025 CAMRAS experiment, and first detection at 2304 MHz by a
non-professional bistatic radar campaign. No citizen science or amateur
detection at 2304 MHz has been reported. A confirmed detection establishes
the first independent ground-based measurement of Venus radar cross section
at 13 cm wavelength outside of professional campaigns, providing a new data
point for surface roughness models.

The secondary goal is frequency comparison between 1299.5 MHz and 2304 MHz
returns from the same conjunction window. The ratio of radar cross sections
at the two frequencies constrains the frequency dependence of Venus surface
scattering, sensitive to surface dielectric properties and roughness scale
relative to wavelength. The March 2025 CAMRAS experiment provides a directly
comparable baseline dataset at 1299.5 MHz.

The third goal is to be the first demonstration of open-source
spread-spectrum digital waveforms via Venus reflection. Open Research
Institute has developed M-ary orthogonal signaling waveforms for this
channel, with Monte Carlo bit error rate simulations suggesting 0.1 to 1.0
information bits per second at the operating carrier-to-noise ratio. At 4
minutes per transmission burst, this yields approximately 120 to 240
information bits. More than sufficient for legal callsign, timestamp, and
message content. Participating stations should be able to close the
communications link. Link budget modeling with Effelsberg as receive station
shows the link closes under coherent integration alone with substantial
margin above the detection threshold. With a variety of 18-25 meter dishes
transmitting and receiving across the campaign, having Effelsberg receive
significantly reduces the risk of failing to close the link at any one
particular smaller receiver.

Why were March 2025 Results Important as a Precedent?

On March 22, 2025, CAMRAS successfully reflected a 1299.5 MHz CW signal off
Venus using the 25m Dwingeloo telescope, with the Stockert 25m telescope as
a secondary receive station. Dr. Daniel Estevez's analysis confirmed
detection of the reflected signal, characterized the Doppler spread as
approximately plus or minus 0.75 Hz, and validated Doppler prediction models
to within a few mHz (Estevez 2025). Stockert's reception, achieved at 13 km
line-of-sight from Effelsberg with a 25-m aperture and linear-to-circular
polarization synthesis, provides an existence proof in Effelsberg's own
geometric and atmospheric context. This result directly validates the
experimental approach proposed here. The October 2026 conjunction offers
another opportunity for improved waveform design, a second frequency, and
substantially greater receive sensitivity through Effelsberg participation.

Why Effelsberg?

The 100m aperture of the Effelsberg telescope provides approximately 12 dB
of receive advantage over the 25m Dwingeloo and Stockert telescopes and
approximately 14 dB over the 20m Bochum telescope. This margin enables
confident detection of structured digital waveforms, not just a carrier
tone, and remains positive across all candidate transmitting stations
including smaller dishes at lower power. This opens the experiment up to a
larger number of citizen scientist participants.

Beyond aperture, Effelsberg offers four specific advantages for this
campaign:

1. European longitude co-location with Dwingeloo. Simultaneous Venus
   visibility from Dwingeloo and Effelsberg spans essentially the full
   European-longitude Venus transit, maximizing the bistatic observing
   window. Orbital geometry during the October 2026 conjunction places Venus
   above 10 degrees elevation at both sites for approximately 6-7 hours
   per day.

2. Existing receiver suite. The P217mm 7-Beam central horn provides dual
   circular polarization (LCP/RCP) at 1299.5 MHz, which is a direct
   polarization match to Dwingeloo's RHCP transmit. The P170mm UBB receiver
   covers 2304 MHz with dual linear polarization, for which the
   linear-to-circular polarization synthesis methodology is well
   established from the March 2025 Stockert analysis.

3. Demonstrated institutional precedent for planetary reception. The
   Effelsberg P500mm was configured for Mars InSight lander signal reception
   in 2018, showing MPIfR has supported NASA planetary reception campaigns.
   Additionally, the 2006 FGAN/TIRA bistatic space-debris campaign used the
   P217mm 7-Beam as the bistatic secondary receiver, demonstrating the exact
   operational mode requested here.

4. RFI-shielded valley location. The Effelsberg site was chosen in the 1960s
   specifically for its natural RFI shielding from surrounding hills, which
   remains effective today. This is particularly valuable at 2304 MHz where
   ISM-band interference is a documented concern at many European sites.
   The 2009 AMSAT-DL Bochum EVE experiment at 2.45 GHz reported microwave
   oven magnetron interference in their recordings. Effelsberg's valley
   geometry substantially mitigates this class of interference.

Receiver Compatibility

P217mm 7-Beam (central horn): 1260-1510 MHz, dual circular polarization
(LCP/RCP), prime focus. Covers 1299.5 MHz with matched polarization to
Dwingeloo's RHCP transmit. With the EDD backend, the central horn alone is
available, which is sufficient for this experiment.

P170mm UBB: 1.3-6.0 GHz, dual linear polarization, prime focus. Covers both
1299.5 MHz and 2304 MHz. For this proposal, UBB is specified for the 2304
MHz sessions. Linear-to-circular polarization synthesis follows the
methodology published for the March 2025 CAMRAS/Stockert experiment.

Open Science Value

All recorded data will be released immediately upon acquisition in SigMF
format under standard open source licensing via the Open Research Institute
data archive. Analysis software, link budget notebooks, ephemeris
calculations, and the Magellan GREDR albedo mapper used to design this
experiment are publicly available at github.com/OpenResearchInstitute/EVE
under open licenses. This experiment engages the international amateur radio
and radio astronomy communities in a scientifically productive planetary
radar campaign.
